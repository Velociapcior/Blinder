Blinder

A slow dating platform built on a structural conviction: genuine human connection begins with personality, not appearance.

01 — Executive Summary

Project Vision

Blinder removes the visual layer from matching and early interaction entirely. Users are matched by values and personality via a short onboarding quiz, enter a real-time chat with a curated match, and unlock a consensual mutual photo reveal only after both parties independently opt in — with a minimum message threshold required first.

Brand North Star

At least one person who uses Blinder believes they are no longer lonely.

Key Design Challenges

  1. Designing for radical unfamiliarity. The no-browse, no-swipe model is a fundamental departure from every existing dating app. Every screen must feel intentional, not broken.
  2. The reveal as a high-stakes emotional moment. Simultaneous exchange, appropriate friction, and a design that honours the courage it took to get there. It cannot feel like clicking a button.
  3. Waiting without anxiety. Empty states and waiting states must feel like reassurance, not rejection.
  4. Onboarding that converts Natalia. The photo upload step — the most anxiety-producing moment — needs exceptional copy and UX treatment to prevent abandonment.
  5. Trust without social proof. The UI itself must communicate trustworthiness without conventional signals (reviews, followers).
02 — Core User Experience

The Core Loop

Defining Interaction

Match → First message → Conversation deepens → Reveal readiness builds → Mutual opt-in → Simultaneous reveal → Continued connection

Critical Success Moments

1

End of Onboarding: "There is already someone waiting for you."

The product must be ready to present a match — or a holding message that feels warm and anticipatory, not like rejection.

2

The First Real Reply

When the other person responds in a way that shows they actually read what was said — the notification design must make this feel significant, not routine.

3

The Mutual Reveal

The waiting state must feel suspenseful and warm, the reveal delivered with ceremony, and both users given a moment to absorb before the next action.

4

Returning After a Reveal

The product must gently acknowledge their return. If the match did not continue, the next-match flow must feel like a fresh start, not a reminder of what didn't work.

5

The Empty State (Match Not Yet Available)

For Marek and Natalia, a waiting screen is high-risk. Must actively communicate: "You have not been forgotten. Your match is being found carefully."

Experience Principles

Principle 1

Personality is the UI

Every element not in direct service of the conversation should be removed or made invisible. Quiet, uncluttered, calm.

Principle 2

Waiting is not failure

Empty states and loading states must be designed with the same care as primary interactions. Reassurance is a first-class UX deliverable.

Principle 3

The reveal deserves ceremony

The mutual photo reveal is the product's signature moment. Every design choice leading to and through the reveal must acknowledge that something significant just happened.

Principle 4

Safety is never hidden

Trust mechanics must be surfaced clearly. Users — especially women — must be able to see how they are protected, not just be promised that they are.

Principle 5

The dropout cohort belongs here

Every screen, every piece of microcopy, every empty state reinforces: this product was built for people who were told the current system isn't for them.

Anti-Patterns to Avoid

  • ✕ The Browse Grid — gallery of profile photos invites snap judgment before conversation can begin. Architecture of objectification. Never used.
  • ✕ The Swipe Mechanic — binary judgment is a dopamine loop, not a connection tool. Nothing in Blinder should echo the swipe. Its absence is a moral position.
  • ✕ Engagement Metrics as Status Signals — "47 new likes." None of these signals exist in Blinder. The only feedback that matters is the quality of the conversation right now.
  • ✕ Inbox-as-Flood — 3-conversation structural limit eliminates harassment. UI must never surface volume as a feature or status signal.
  • ✕ Premium Urgency Gating — "Upgrade to see who liked you." Blinder's premium never gates expected access. Upgrade = capability expansion, not hostage-taking.
  • ✕ Precise Activity Status — "Last seen 2 minutes ago" transforms anticipation into dread. Status indicators must be warm and vague only.
  • ✕ Micro-Reward Mechanics — daily streaks, reaction counts, visitor logs recreate the Tinder dopamine architecture Blinder is designed to replace.
05 — Design System Foundation

Design System

Blinder uses a Themeable System with Design Token Layer — primary library NativeWind (Tailwind CSS for React Native) or Tamagui. Battle-tested component primitives; brand expression fully owned through tokens.

Framework Decision Required

NativeWind — lower learning curve for Tailwind-familiar teams. Tamagui — more powerful; better long-term fit for bespoke animation (reveal motion). Decision must not be deferred past Sprint 1.

Colour System — "Well-lit Room at Dusk"

Warm, intimate, and calm. Not bright primary colours. Not aggressive reds. Not cold greys. Dark mode is primary — dating apps are used at night.

Amber Bookend Principle

The amber accent appears at two emotional peaks — the very beginning (onboarding entry, within the first 3 seconds) and the climax (reveal trigger). This creates a visual through-line users feel subconsciously.

The Reveal Arc

The reveal is not a single screen — it is a 5-phase arc. Every phase has a distinct design requirement.

1
Readiness Build-Up
2
Solo Indication
3
Waiting State
4
Mutual Trigger & Reveal
5
Continuation Gate
Phase 1 — Readiness Build-Up (In-Conversation)

A subtle, non-intrusive reveal affordance quietly appears in the conversation when the message threshold is reached. Not a push notification. Not a modal. A door that has quietly opened.

"You've had a real conversation. The reveal is available when you're both ready."

Phase 2 — Readiness Indication (Solo Action)

Full-screen transition (not a modal). Explanation surfaced here with structural consent architecture visible. "Not yet" option is visually equal to "ready" — no dark patterns nudging toward readiness.

Phase 3 — Waiting State

One party ready, one not yet. Must never feel like being stood up. Both parties on the eve of something — shared patience. No timer, no read receipt equivalent.

"You've indicated you're ready. Waiting for them."

Phase 4 — Mutual Trigger & Reveal Delivery ★ Most Important Screen

System detects mutual readiness. Both devices receive a simultaneous transition. Full-screen reveal moment. 600–800ms motion treatment — long enough to feel momentous, short enough to not feel like buffering. 1–2 seconds of space before anything interactive appears.

Phase 5 — Post-Reveal Continuation Gate

Both users independently asked: "Would you like to keep talking?" Neither sees the other's answer until both respond. Asymmetric outcome protected — dignity preserved for both parties.

Accessibility Foundation

  • Minimum tap target: 44×44px for all interactive elements.
  • Contrast compliance: All text/background pairs meet WCAG AA; primary content pairs meet AAA.
  • Dynamic type: Typography scale responds to system text size settings. Minimum sizes never drop below 11px at default scale.
  • Colour-independent signalling: Safety indicators paired with iconography or text labels — never colour alone.
  • Reduced motion: Reveal animation respects "reduce motion" — falls back to a cross-fade rather than ceremonial motion.
  • Screen reader: Reveal arc screens have carefully considered VoiceOver/TalkBack copy — the emotional moment communicated through accessible text, not only visual design.
